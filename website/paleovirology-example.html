A CRESS-GLUE example for paleovirologists

This page provides a demonstration of CRESS-GLUE being used to (i) organise the fossil record and; (ii) expand the fossil record to accomodate new data.

Organising the fossil record

We used DIGS to identify the complete repertoire of ECV sequences in published vertebrate genome assemblies. Through comparative analysis of these sequences we could establish a picture yet of how viruses in the genus Circovirus have evolved and interacted with their hosts over the course of their evolution.

ECV have previously been identified in the genome of the short-tailed opossum (Monodelphis domestica), an American marsupial. In phylogenies based on rep, this sequence groups together with the porcine circoviruses, canine circovirus, and an ECV identified in the Steppe mouse (Mus caroli). We identified the first examples of ECV from the genomes of Australian marsupial species: the Tasmanian devil (Sarcophilus harrisii) and the koala (Phascolarctos cinereus). Both these sequences derived from circovirus rep genes, and grouped together in phylogenetic trees. However, their placement relative to other taxa was not supported with confidence, reflecting their short and degraded nature. Several other short and degraded matches to Rep probes were identified in other mammalian species. These sequences were relatively distantly related to one another and to contemporary circoviruses.
